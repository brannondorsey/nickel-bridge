NICKEL BRIDGE Onboarding · Concept Board · Three Directions

The first crossing

Today a newcomer goes splash → handle → "The bridge is open." — with no idea what a crossing is, why everyone plays the same deals, or that the app teaches while you play. The tour has three things to teach, in order of difficulty: duplicate (the same-deals ledger — the whole point, and the least familiar idea), the teaching loop (bid meanings before you commit, grades after), and the philosophy (a small unhurried club; one crossing at a time; judgment over luck). Each concept below covers all three — they differ in how the player receives them: reading, playing, or conversing.

All frames at 390×844 reference, real house voice, design-system components only. Copy is proposed final, not lorem.

Concept A

The Crossing Pamphlet

read it — a printed leaflet from the toll office

A five-panel swipe sequence styled as the pamphlet the tollkeeper hands you at the gate. Pure exposition, but set like a period artifact: numbered panels, perforation-dot pager, an ink stamp pressed on each panel as you pass. Ends by asking the one question the backend already supports but no UI asks — your difficulty preference — so even the passive version pays its way. Cheapest to build; leaves a permanent, revisitable artifact (the pamphlet stays reachable from the gate/glossary).

I · THE BRIDGE
A small club,
one crossing at a time.

Nickel Bridge is a club for learning bridge by playing it. No timers, no strangers, no lobby of a thousand tables. You sit South, always. Your partner is a robot of even temper; your opponents, two more.

The people you're truly playing are your friends — who cross behind you, on the very same cards.

Named for the 1925 toll bridge over the James River: a dime to cross, then a nickel, now fifty cents. The name outlived the price.

Concept B

Board №0 — A Practice Crossing

play it — one guided deal in the real Board UI

The tour is a hand of bridge: a scripted, deterministic practice board played through the real gameplay screen, with the tollkeeper narrating in a ribbon above the action. You genuinely bid (meaning panel moment), get genuinely graded (toast moment), play a trick or two, and then the ledger reveal teaches duplicate where it actually lives — the field table, pre-populated by the three house personas who "crossed before you." Nothing scored, nothing rated. Ends with a FIRST CROSSING postmark. Most build effort — but the scripted-replay machinery already exists (demo exhibits, scenarios.ts, exhibit-kind tournaments excluded from Elo/stats/placement), so the hard part is largely written.

Concept C

The Tollkeeper's Window

converse — short exchanges at the booth, each with a small proof

You've pulled up to the booth window (the double-ink "table" frame) and the tollkeeper talks to you — one idea per exchange, answered with a button. Each claim carries a small interactive proof instead of a paragraph: a tap-a-bid-see-its-meaning demo, a four-crossings-one-ledger diagram, the difficulty question as the toll itself. Warmer than A, far lighter than B; the conversation frame also gives skipping a in-world shape ("I know the way").

RECOMMENDATION

"A tour worth taking" points at B as the spine: the only concept where the player does the thing rather than being told about it, and the only one where duplicate — the app's whole thesis — is taught inside the very component that embodies it. Wrap it in the best of the others: C's tollkeeper as B's narrator voice (one character, met at the gate, who rides along on Board №0), and A's gate panel as the closing screen — difficulty question, then PLAY THE TOLL. A condensed pamphlet survives as the revisitable "HOW TO CROSS" reference reachable from the glossary, so the tour can be short without being lossy. Build-wise the practice board rides on machinery that already exists (exhibit-kind tournaments, scripted replays, house personas), and the whole flow needs a demo-mode exhibit anyway per house rules.
